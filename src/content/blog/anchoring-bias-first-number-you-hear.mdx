The foreman glances at the trench and says, "Looks about four feet." Nobody pulls out a tape measure. The number is out there now, floating in everyone's head. When the safety manager walks by an hour later and asks how deep it is, three different guys all say "around four feet." Nobody actually measured. They're all just repeating the first number they heard. The trench is five feet two inches. Shoring was required. Nobody noticed because the anchor was already set.

## The Number That Sticks

This is anchoring bias, and it runs every jobsite you've ever worked. The first piece of information you hear becomes the reference point for everything that follows. Amos Tversky and Daniel Kahneman identified this in 1974 — people make estimates by starting from an initial value and adjusting from there. The problem is we don't adjust enough. We stay close to that first number, even when it's completely wrong.

On a site, this happens constantly. Someone eyeballs a load and says "probably 800 pounds," and now everyone treats it like 800 pounds. Someone estimates a confined space has "plenty of ventilation," and the crew stops questioning whether the air is actually safe. The first assessment becomes the accepted reality, even when nobody verified it.

## Why Measuring Feels Like Doubting

Here's what makes anchoring so dangerous in construction: pulling out a tape measure after someone has already given an estimate feels like you're challenging them. It feels like you're saying they're wrong. So people don't do it. They accept the anchor and move on. Adrian Furnham and Hua Chu Boo reviewed decades of anchoring research in 2011 and found that even obviously random anchors influence judgment. Even when people know the initial number is arbitrary, it still affects their estimates.

The trench foreman wasn't trying to mislead anyone. He made a quick visual estimate, which is a normal human thing to do. But once he said it out loud, that number became the crew's shared reality. Nobody wanted to be the guy who questioned it.

## The First Voice Sets the Standard

This is why the order of safety observations matters more than most people realize. If the first person to comment on a setup says "looks good," everyone else is now anchored to "looks good." They'll notice fewer problems. They'll rationalize what they see to match that initial judgment. Nicholas Epley and Thomas Gilovich found in 2006 that people anchor even on their own previous estimates — we're not just influenced by what others say, but by what we ourselves said earlier.

On a crew, this compounds. The first risk assessment sets the tone. The first estimate sets the range. The first opinion about whether a task is safe becomes the default that everyone else has to argue against.

## Breaking the Anchor

The fix isn't complicated, but it requires deliberate effort. Measure before estimating. Get multiple independent observations before discussing. Don't let the first voice be the only voice that matters. And when possible, bring in a perspective that hasn't heard the anchor yet — someone who looks at the conditions fresh, without inheriting the crew's existing assumptions.

The goal isn't to doubt every foreman who calls a depth. It's to recognize that the first number you hear pulls every estimate after it. Sometimes that first number is right. Sometimes it's four feet when you need to know it's five.
