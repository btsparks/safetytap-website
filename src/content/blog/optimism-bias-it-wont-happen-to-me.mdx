## The Guy Who Never Falls

There's a guy on your crew — probably more than one — who hasn't fallen in twenty years. He walks steel at height like it's a sidewalk. He sets anchors when he feels like it, not because some checklist says to. He's fast, he's careful in his own way, and nothing bad has ever happened to him.

He thinks that means something about him.

It does. But not what he thinks.

## The Belief That Experience Equals Immunity

Most people believe experienced workers are safer. They've seen the hazards. They know the tricks. They've developed judgment. All of that is true. But there's something else happening that nobody talks about — a psychological mechanism that makes those same experienced workers more vulnerable, not less.

It's called optimism bias, and it's the quiet conviction that bad things happen to other people. Not to you.

Neil Weinstein studied this in 1980 and found that people consistently believe they are less likely than their peers to experience negative events — car accidents, illness, job loss. Even when you show them the statistics. Even when they know the base rates. They still think: Yeah, but that's not me.

Tali Sharot's research on the brain's optimism bias shows why. Our brains are wired to discount negative information about our own future. When we hear about someone else's accident, we process it. When we imagine our own risk, the brain actually suppresses the warning signals. It feels like realism. It's not.

Daniel Kahneman called this the planning fallacy — the systematic tendency to underestimate how long things will take and how likely things are to go wrong. Experienced people are worse at this, not better. Because they have a library of memories that say: I handled it before.

## Why the Best Ironworker Is at the Highest Risk

Here's what makes this dangerous on a construction site: Optimism bias is strongest in people who feel skilled and in control.

The ironworker with twenty years and no serious falls doesn't just feel confident. He feels statistically safer. His brain uses his personal history as evidence. Every day he doesn't fall reinforces the belief that he won't. His skill is real. His belief that skill makes him immune to probability is not.

The data tells a different story. Experienced workers account for a significant portion of serious injuries and fatalities. Not because they're careless. Because they trust their own judgment more than the system, and their judgment is quietly, invisibly biased.

OSHA recently cited a Florida HVAC contractor after a worker was killed in a struck-by incident — one of the Fatal Four. These aren't obscure hazards. Struck-by deaths happen on sites every week. But workers who've spent years around moving equipment and rigging loads stop seeing the risk. They've done it a thousand times. They have a sense for when something's wrong. Except the brain's sense for risk is not a reliable instrument. It's an optimism engine.

## The Invisible Killers Are Worse

Optimism bias gets even more dangerous with hazards you can't see. A Florida petroleum tank services contractor was recently cited by OSHA after a worker died from toxic chemical exposure. Confined space work. Chemical atmospheres. These are known, controlled hazards. But when you've been in and out of tanks for years without incident, your brain stops treating the risk as real.

You remember all the times it was fine. You don't remember that you were fine because of systems that were working — ventilation, air monitoring, protocols. You think you were fine because you know what you're doing. So when the system isn't there, you still feel safe. That feeling is optimism bias wearing the mask of experience.

The problem isn't that workers don't know the hazards exist. It's that their brains are designed to believe those hazards apply to someone else.

## What Cuts Through It

You can't fix optimism bias with more training. Training reinforces the feeling of competence, which feeds the bias. You can't fix it by showing people statistics. Statistics are about other people.

What works is external, objective observation that doesn't care what you believe about yourself.

Digital twin technology is starting to show this — systems that model jobsites and identify hazards before anyone steps into the space. The value isn't just prediction. It's that the hazard assessment comes from outside the worker's head. It doesn't run through the optimism filter.

The twenty-year ironworker's brain will tell him he's got it under control. A camera sees what's actually there — unsecured edges, shifting loads, proximity to power lines. It doesn't care about his track record.

No app, no login, just an external check that bypasses the brain's tendency to edit out risk. It's not about doubting skill. It's about not trusting the part of the brain that thinks skill equals immunity.

## The Paradox Nobody Wants to Say Out Loud

The best workers are often the most at risk. Not because they're reckless. Because their brains reward them for years of success by turning down the volume on warnings. That's not a character flaw. It's how human cognition works.

The question isn't whether your crew is skilled. It's whether you're building a system that works even when skill makes people feel safer than they are.
