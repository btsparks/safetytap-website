The rigger walks the perimeter one more time. The crane's positioned. Load's within capacity. Ground's been checked. Everything on the lift plan says go. But he stops at the tag line and doesn't reach for it. His superintendent notices.

"What's up?"

"I don't know. Something doesn't feel right."

That moment happens on sites everywhere. A veteran operator refuses a pour. A foreman waves off a delivery. An ironworker backs away from a connection. No specific reason they can articulate. Just a feeling. And everyone else on the crew has to decide whether to listen.

## When feelings become facts

Psychologist Paul Slovic spent decades studying how people judge risk, and what he found should matter to anyone running a crew. When we decide if something is dangerous, we rarely do the math. We don't calculate probabilities or compare incident rates. We feel our way to an answer.

Slovic called this the affect heuristic. If an activity feels bad, we see it as high risk. If it feels fine, we see it as low risk. The feeling comes first. The reasoning follows behind, building a case for what we've already decided.

On a jobsite, this happens constantly. A lift that feels sketchy gets more scrutiny. A task that feels routine gets waved through. The crew who likes the new sub thinks their work is solid. The crew who doesn't trust them finds problems everywhere.

The critical question isn't whether gut feelings influence safety decisions. They absolutely do. The question is whether those feelings are telling us something real.

## Two kinds of knowing

Gary Klein studied firefighters, nurses, and military commanders — people who make high-stakes decisions under pressure with incomplete information. He found that experts don't compare options or weigh pros and cons. They look at a situation and immediately know what to do. The building's going to collapse. The patient's crashing. The enemy's in that tree line.

Klein called this recognition-primed decision making. Pattern recognition happening faster than conscious thought. The expert has seen thousands of similar situations. Their brain has built a massive library of patterns. When something matches, they know. When something's off, they feel it.

This is real expertise. The rigger with twenty years recognizing that the load's going to swing differently than the plan suggests. The crane operator feeling the ground respond wrong under the outriggers. The superintendent sensing that the schedule pressure is pushing decisions too fast.

But Daniel Kahneman, who spent his career documenting how systematic and predictable our reasoning errors are, pushed back on Klein's work. They ended up writing a paper together in 2009, trying to figure out when intuition works and when it fails. Their conclusion matters for construction.

Intuition is only reliable in environments where patterns repeat and where you get clear, immediate feedback about whether your decisions were right. A poker player develops real intuition because they play thousands of hands and see the results immediately. A stock picker doesn't, because the feedback is delayed, noisy, and the patterns don't actually repeat in predictable ways.

Construction sits somewhere in between. The structural engineer who's designed hundreds of connections has built reliable intuition about how loads transfer. The formwork carpenter who's stripped and reset forms on dozens of pours knows how concrete sets and where weak points develop. But the new safety manager walking a site for the first time? That bad feeling might be expertise, or it might just be nerves.

## What corrupts the signal

Gerd Gigerenzer researched what he calls the intelligence of the unconscious — the sophisticated processing that happens below awareness. But he also documented how easily that processing gets hijacked.

Your current mood bleeds into your risk assessment. The rigger who had an argument with his wife that morning sees more danger. The foreman celebrating a project milestone sees less. Neither realizes their judgment is being influenced by something completely unrelated to the work.

Recent experience dominates. If someone on your crew got hurt last week, every task feels more dangerous for a while. If you've run the same pick fifty times without incident, this one feels routine even if conditions have changed.

Familiarity masks risk. The crew working the same site for months stops seeing hazards they noticed the first week. The task doesn't feel dangerous anymore, so it stops getting the scrutiny it needs.

This isn't a failure of the person. It's how the affect heuristic works. Feelings are fast and usually functional, but they're systematically influenced by factors that have nothing to do with actual risk.

## Making feelings useful

The worst response to all this is to dismiss gut feelings entirely. Tell your crew to ignore their instincts and just follow procedures. That's how you lose the signal along with the noise.

The veteran who feels something's wrong is often detecting a real problem their conscious mind hasn't articulated yet. The equipment that looks fine on paper but feels off might have a subtle issue you'll only catch if you investigate. Intuition developed through thousands of hours of quality feedback is genuine expertise.

But you can't just trust every bad feeling either. The anxious worker who thinks everything's dangerous isn't displaying expertise. The crew who waves off legitimate concerns because the work feels familiar isn't either.

The answer is treating gut feelings as information that needs to be investigated, not as conclusions. When something feels wrong, that's a signal to look closer. Document what you're seeing. Get a second set of eyes. Figure out if there's a concrete hazard your experience is detecting, or if the feeling is coming from somewhere else.

Sometimes that investigation confirms the concern. The rigger couldn't articulate what felt wrong about the lift, but when you walk it again you notice the load's shifted slightly in the packaging. The ground near the outrigger looked fine but when you probe it you find it's softer than the compaction test suggested. The feeling was expertise, and stopping was the right call.

Other times the investigation comes back clean. The equipment's solid. The plan's sound. The conditions haven't changed. Now you can proceed with confidence, because you've ruled out the concrete hazards and you know the feeling was anxiety, not pattern recognition.

## Learning which voice to listen to

The rigger who won't make the lift needs to know whether his hesitation is wisdom or worry. His superintendent needs to know whether to back him up or coach him through it. The only way to develop that judgment is to close the loop. When someone has a bad feeling, investigate. Then see what you find.

Over time, you learn whose gut feelings predict real problems and whose reflect general anxiety. You learn which situations trigger false alarms and which ones your crew isn't worried enough about. You build the feedback loops that let intuition become reliable.

Your best people already do this. They've learned to distinguish the feeling of genuine danger from the feeling of discomfort. They've calibrated their instincts through years of discovering what was actually wrong and what just felt wrong.

The question is whether you're building that capability across your crew or just hoping they figure it out on their own.

When the rigger says something feels wrong, the right answer isn't "trust your gut" or "stick to the plan." It's "show me what you're seeing." Turn the feeling into information. Make the implicit explicit. That's how gut feelings become useful instead of just being one more voice in your head.
