The excavation foreman pauses at the edge of the trench. Six feet down, two workers are placing formwork. A mini-excavator idles twenty feet back. An unmarked section of ground runs diagonal through the work area — the utility locate expired three days ago. He's not running through a checklist in his head. He's scanning for energy.

Stored energy in the walls that could collapse inward. Kinetic energy from the machine that could swing or roll. Electrical energy from whatever's buried in that unmarked zone. He's thinking: where's the energy, what releases it, who gets hit?

This is energy-based thinking, and it's how experienced workers actually recognize hazards on site. Not by memorizing lists or reciting compliance steps, but by developing an instinct for one fundamental question: what energy is present here, and what's between it and a person?

## Every Injury Is an Energy Transfer

In 1970, public health researcher William Haddon published a paper with an unforgettable title: "On the Escape of Tigers: An Ecologic Note." His premise was simple: injuries happen when energy contacts the human body in amounts or rates the body can't handle. A fall is gravitational energy transfer. An electrocution is electrical energy transfer. A struck-by incident is kinetic energy transfer.

Haddon's insight changed how researchers understood injury causation, but it never quite made it to the jobsite in a form workers could use. Safety programs still hand out hazard lists organized by task or trade — excavation hazards, roofing hazards, electrical hazards. Workers dutifully memorize them and then stand in a work area trying to match what they see to what they memorized.

Energy-based thinking flips this. Instead of starting with categories, you start with physics. You look at your work area and identify the energy sources: gravitational, kinetic, electrical, thermal, chemical, pressure. Then you trace the pathways. What could release this energy? What's controlling it right now? If that control fails, who's in the path?

## The Trench That Teaches

Consider OSHA's recent citation of Revoli Construction after a fatal trench collapse. Seven willful violations. Thirty-three repeat violations. The company had been cited before. They knew the regulations. They'd been through the compliance drill. And a worker still died when stored energy in the soil walls released and contacted his body.

This wasn't a knowledge problem. It was a thinking problem. Somewhere in the chain of decisions leading to that trench, no one paused to ask: what energy is present, what's controlling it, and is that control adequate? The energy was obvious — tons of soil held temporarily in place by friction and cohesion. The control was inadequate — no shoring, no sloping, no protection. The pathway was clear — workers in the trench.

Energy-based thinking makes this visible before the collapse, not after. It turns hazard recognition into pattern recognition, which is something the human brain does instinctively. You don't need to remember whether OSHA requires shoring at six feet or seven feet. You need to see stored energy and ask if the barrier between it and the crew is solid.

## The Roofing Edge

The same pattern shows up in OSHA's citation of a Florida roofing company after a fatal fall. One worker dead, another injured, the company cited for willful exposure. Falls are gravitational energy — mass times acceleration. The energy source is constant: the height of the working surface. The control is what varies: guardrails, personal fall arrest, warning lines, something between the worker and the edge.

When the Department of Labor promotes National Safety Stand-Down campaigns focused on fall prevention, they're asking crews to stop and think. But stop and think about what? Energy-based thinking gives workers something concrete to do with that pause. Look at the roof edge. The energy is gravitational potential — your mass, the distance to the ground. What's controlling it? What happens if that control fails? Are you in the pathway?

This isn't theoretical. It's the same mental process an experienced roofer uses every time they step onto a slope. They're not reciting fall protection regulations. They're reading the energy landscape.

## Scanning for Energy

The energy model works across every trade because energy doesn't care about trade boundaries. A plumber opening a pressurized line, an electrician working near an energized panel, a carpenter standing under a suspended load — they're all managing energy pathways. The specific energy type changes. The mental model doesn't.

Where's the energy? Gravitational in the load overhead. Electrical in the panel. Pressure in the pipe. What's controlling it? Rigging and the crane operator. Panel cover and lockout. A valve and maybe some residual pressure. What releases it? Rigging failure, operator error, someone opening the panel, someone opening the valve. Who's in the path? Anyone standing under, reaching in, standing in front.

This is how SafetyTAP's observation framework approaches a photo. The AI isn't matching images to a database of violations. It's identifying uncontrolled energy — a suspended load with someone underneath, an exposed conductor, unsecured material on an elevated surface. The technology maps the energy landscape the same way an experienced foreman does, then flags the pathway between energy source and worker.

## The Question That Sticks

Energy-based thinking doesn't replace compliance. You still need to know the standards, still need proper equipment, still need protective systems. But it changes how workers see the site. Instead of walking through a work area thinking "am I following the rules," they're thinking "where's the energy and what happens if it gets loose?"

That's the difference between checking boxes and actually recognizing hazards. One is external — did I do the thing I'm supposed to do? The other is internal — do I see the physics of this situation? The first fades the moment the safety manager walks away. The second becomes instinct.
