The new scaffold erector has watched the competent person inspect the structure. She's seen the tie-in sequence, the plank placement protocol, the load distribution check. Thirty minutes of clear demonstration. Then the competent person climbs down, nods, and says, "You're good. Crew's waiting on the east side." The erector heads over, tools in hand, and suddenly realizes she has no idea what to do first when something looks slightly different from the demo.

We call this training. But we've skipped the part where learning actually happens.

## The Missing Middle of Competence

In 1983, P. David Pearson and Margaret Gallagher described what effective instruction actually requires: a gradual release of responsibility. Their model identified three distinct phases. First, the expert demonstrates while explaining their thinking — the "I do" phase. Then comes guided practice, where the learner performs while the expert stays close, offering real-time feedback and correction — the "We do" phase. Finally, independent practice, where the learner works alone but can still access support — the "You do" phase.

Construction safety training routinely jumps from the first phase to the third. We demonstrate fall protection procedures in the morning toolbox talk. Then we send crews up to tie off independently by lunch. The middle phase, where a superintendent works alongside the new crew member and says "Look at that connection point — what do you notice about the angle?" gets compressed to nothing. Production pressure makes that gradual transition feel like a luxury we can't afford.

Except it's not a luxury. It's where competence gets built.

This year's National Safety Stand-Down to Prevent Falls — OSHA's annual campaign encouraging employers to pause work and discuss fall hazards — works precisely because it creates that middle space. For a few hours, crews aren't alone with the hazard. They're thinking about it together, asking questions, getting immediate feedback. The stand-down is structured guided practice, even if we don't call it that. The problem is it happens once a year, for falls specifically, and then we go back to the jump-from-demo-to-independent model for everything else.

## What Scaffolding Actually Means

In 1976, psychologist David Wood and his colleagues studied how tutors help learners solve problems they couldn't handle alone. They called it scaffolding — temporary support structures that hold up the learner's performance until they can stand independently. Good scaffolding does more than demonstrate the right answer. It stays present during the struggle, adjusting support based on what the learner needs in that specific moment.

Lev Vygotsky described this as working within someone's zone of proximal development — the space between what they can do alone and what they can do with guidance. Too far outside that zone and they're lost. But right in the zone, with the right scaffolding, they grow.

On site, that looks like an experienced foreman standing beside a new laborer as they set up a ladder, not doing it for them, but asking: "What's the angle look like to you? How are you checking stability? Where's your three-point contact?" The learner does the work. The expert provides the cognitive structure.

The BCSP and NCCER recently partnered to integrate safety credentials with workforce development programs, recognizing that formal training pathways need built-in scaffolding. The partnership acknowledges that a safety credential isn't just a certificate — it represents structured progression through increasingly complex safety decisions, with support at each level. That's the instructional model made formal.

## Why the Middle Phase Disappears

Production schedules don't budget for the "We do" phase. A superintendent who could shadow a new crew member through their first week of independent hazard scanning is instead managing three other sites. The competent person who could provide real-time coaching on scaffold inspection is already twenty minutes behind on the next setup.

So we optimize for speed. We give the demonstration, we hand over the responsibility, and we hope the worker figures out the middle part on their own. Some do. Many don't. And when an incident happens, we point back to the demonstration they received and conclude they weren't paying attention.

New York's Deadly Skyline report showed construction worker fatalities dropped 26% in 2024 after a ten-year high the previous year. That's significant. But the interesting question isn't just the reduction — it's what changed in that year to produce it. Better regulations help. Improved equipment matters. But sustained drops in fatalities usually trace back to changes in how workers develop recognition capability. Something shifted in how crews learned to see hazards, and that shift likely included more supported practice, more coaching in the moment, more scaffolding in the middle phase where competence actually forms.

## Building the Support Structure In

The challenge for safety managers is finding a way to provide guided practice at scale. You can't personally coach every crew member through every hazard scenario. But you can build systems that function as scaffolding — tools that let workers practice hazard recognition while still receiving expert-level feedback.

That's the structural principle behind SafetyTAP. A crew member scans their work area and texts a photo. They're practicing the recognition skill independently — the "You do" phase — but they immediately receive a specific safety observation from an expert perspective. It's not correction after an incident. It's real-time guidance during the learning process. The worker's scanning improves because the scaffolding is present when they need it, then gradually becomes less necessary as their own recognition develops.

The gradual release model doesn't ask you to slow down production. It asks you to recognize that competence requires a supported middle phase, and to build that support into how work actually happens. The scaffold erector who gets real-time coaching doesn't take longer to become competent. She becomes competent faster, and she stays that way.
