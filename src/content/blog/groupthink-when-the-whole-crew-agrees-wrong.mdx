The foreman watches as the crew aligns the 40-foot W24 beam into position. "We need the temporary brace?" someone asks. The foreman looks at the connection, looks at the rest of the crew. Everyone's done this detail dozens of times. "Nah," he says. "This beam sits solid once the bolts go in. We're good." The ironworker nods. The apprentice, who's been wondering about the wind picking up, says nothing. Within three minutes, everyone's convinced it's fine. Within ten minutes, a gust catches the beam at just the wrong angle.

Nobody made a reckless decision. Nobody ignored a rule out of laziness. Five experienced professionals looked at the same situation and arrived at the same wrong conclusion together. They felt certain because they all agreed. That's exactly what made it dangerous.

## The Comfort of Consensus

Psychologist Irving Janis spent years studying how intelligent, well-informed groups make catastrophically bad decisions. His 1972 research into foreign policy failures identified a pattern he called groupthink — a mode of thinking where the desire for unanimity overrides the realistic evaluation of alternatives. The group feels cohesive, confident, and aligned. That feeling of solidarity becomes more important than the quality of the decision itself.

The symptoms show up clearly on construction sites. An illusion of invulnerability develops: "We've done this a hundred times." Collective rationalization sets in: "The beam's balanced, the bolts go in fast, we're not gonna get a wind event in the next five minutes." And most dangerously, dissenting viewpoints get suppressed — not through hostility, but through social pressure. The apprentice who wonders about the brace doesn't speak up because everyone else seems sure. The ironworker who briefly considers the wind forecast stays quiet because the decision's already been made.

Diane Vaughan's examination of the Challenger disaster revealed how NASA engineers normalized increasingly risky decisions through group processes. Each successful launch with eroding O-rings reinforced the collective belief that the risk was acceptable. The dissenting engineers who raised concerns were seen as overly cautious, not team players. The group's confidence grew with each near-miss, right up until the catastrophe. Construction crews face the same dynamic every day, just at smaller scale.

## When Speaking Up Feels Impossible

Recent research from Mental Health First Aid England surveyed 2,000 workers and found a direct correlation between psychological safety — the ability to speak up without fear — and physical work performance. When people believe their concerns will be dismissed or held against them, they stop raising them. The hazard doesn't disappear. It just stops being discussed.

Construction amplifies this problem. Crews work in tight units where social cohesion matters for efficiency and morale. Nobody wants to be the person who slows down the job or questions the foreman's judgment. The apprentice learning the trade has even less room to challenge the plan. A recent Department of Labor lawsuit against a South Dakota construction company illustrates how deep this runs. An employee reported a workplace injury and was fired in apparent retaliation. If workers face consequences for reporting injuries that already happened, how likely are they to speak up about hazards that might happen?

The answer is they don't. They read the social cues, assess the group consensus, and keep their concerns internal. Then they rationalize those concerns away because everyone else seems confident. That's not weakness. That's normal human psychology in a system that punishes dissent.

## The Dangerous Middle Ground

Groupthink doesn't produce obviously stupid decisions. It produces decisions that seem completely reasonable to everyone involved at the time. Cass Sunstein and Reid Hastie's research on group decision-making shows that groups often become more confident and more extreme in their judgments than individuals would be alone. The crew that skips the temporary brace isn't ignoring safety. They're performing a risk assessment, and the group dynamic is skewing that assessment toward overconfidence.

The problem intensifies with experience. Veteran crews have a deep well of successful past decisions to draw from. "We've always done it this way and nothing's happened" becomes powerful evidence that the approach is sound. Each time the group makes a similar call and nothing goes wrong, it reinforces the pattern. The near-misses — the times when the beam shifted slightly, when the wind almost picked up, when the connection took longer than expected — get forgotten or reframed as proof that the crew knows what they're doing.

But conditions change. The beam is longer this time. The wind is stronger. The bolt holes don't quite line up and the connection takes an extra two minutes. Small variables that would warrant reconsideration in an individual assessment get smoothed over in the group consensus. Everyone's looking at everyone else for confirmation, and everyone's seeing confidence, so the confidence multiplies.

## Breaking the Loop

The solution isn't to eliminate group decision-making. Construction requires crew coordination and collective judgment. The solution is to interrupt the closed loop where the group only hears from itself.

Effective crews build in structured dissent. Someone gets assigned to play devil's advocate, not as a personality trait but as a role. The question "what could go wrong here?" gets asked even when everyone's confident. Pre-task planning that forces the crew to verbalize assumptions makes hidden risks visible. These approaches work when they're systematic, not when they depend on someone feeling brave enough to speak up.

The most reliable intervention is external input that isn't subject to the same social pressures. When an outside perspective reviews the plan without investment in the group's cohesion or history, it can spot the gaps that groupthink conceals. That perspective doesn't need to overrule the crew's judgment. It just needs to surface the question that nobody wanted to ask.

Do your crews have a way to hear from someone who doesn't feel the pull toward consensus? Or does every safety decision get made by people who all want to agree with each other?
