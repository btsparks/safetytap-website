## When Compliance Theater Becomes the Actual Hazard

A superintendent watches his foreman redirect a material delivery truck to a different staging area than what's marked on the site plan. The engineer designed it according to code. The layout met all the requirements. But the foreman knows that semi would block the sightline at the exact moment the crane swings lumber over the pedestrian route. So he moves it.

In a traditional safety program, that's a deviation. Someone didn't follow the plan. In Safety Differently, that's expertise worth understanding.

Most construction safety programs operate on a simple premise: workers are the problem, and rules are the solution. Incident happens, add a rule. Another incident, add another rule. Eventually you have seventy-three pages of procedures and crews who've learned to work around them because following every rule exactly would make the job impossible. Sidney Dekker calls this Safety-I — preventing things from going wrong by controlling worker behavior. It sounds logical until you realize it treats the people who actually do the work as obstacles to manage rather than resources to develop.

The Department of Labor recently partnered with Mascaro Construction on a Heinz History Center expansion project, framing it as a promotional safety effort. On paper, it looks like standard regulatory collaboration. But here's what makes it psychologically interesting: when regulators and contractors work together from the start rather than after incidents, it changes how crews perceive safety observations. It stops feeling like policing and starts feeling like problem-solving. Workers share information differently when they're not worried about being written up. That shift from enforcement to learning is the heart of what Erik Hollnagel calls Safety-II.

Safety-II flips the question. Instead of asking why things go wrong, it asks: how do things go right most of the time? How do crews successfully navigate equipment breakdowns, schedule compression, incomplete drawings, unexpected site conditions, and all the other daily chaos that construction actually involves? What adaptations do they make that keep work moving safely?

Consider trenching. OSHA investigated two recent incidents where workers died in trench collapses — one in Alabama, another involving Revoli Construction, which racked up seven willful and thirty-three repeat violations. Repeat violations. That phrase should stop us cold. It means inspectors found the same problems, wrote the same citations, and nothing changed. A Safety-I response is predictable: more training, stricter enforcement, additional documentation requirements. But Hollnagel's research on resilience engineering suggests that repeat violations reveal something deeper than noncompliance. They reveal that the formal system and the actual work are misaligned.

Workers at Revoli weren't ignoring trench safety because they wanted to cut corners. They were doing what made sense within the constraints they faced — time pressure, available equipment, crew size, soil conditions that don't match the engineer's assumptions. Dekker describes this as the gap between work-as-imagined and work-as-done. The procedures describe an idealized version of excavation where everything goes according to plan. The crew deals with reality, which involves sandy soil that behaves differently than expected and steel plates that don't fit quite right.

A Safety Differently approach doesn't excuse violations. It asks: what were the crew trying to accomplish, what obstacles did they face, and what made the unsafe method seem like the reasonable choice in that moment? Then it asks: how do other crews successfully manage those same pressures without the collapse?

## What Workers Already Know

Your experienced operators make dozens of micro-adjustments every day that prevent incidents. They reposition materials. They sequence tasks differently than planned. They communicate informally because the formal communication process takes too long. Hollnagel calls this adaptive capacity — the ability to adjust to changing conditions and still maintain safety.

Traditional safety management treats those adaptations as deviations. Safety Differently treats them as intelligence. The foreman who rearranges the staging area isn't creating a problem. He's solving one that the engineered solution missed. The question isn't whether to allow that adaptation. It's already happening. The question is whether your safety system learns from it.

This requires a different relationship with your crew. Instead of assuming workers need to be controlled, you assume they have practical knowledge worth capturing. Instead of asking "did you follow the procedure," you ask "what made that the better approach?" Instead of treating near-misses as failures, you treat them as successful recoveries worth understanding.

## From Policing to Learning

The shift sounds philosophical, but it shows up in daily practices. After an incident, do you investigate to find who messed up, or to understand how the system set them up for that choice? When a crew deviates from a plan, do you immediately enforce compliance, or first understand what conditions made the deviation necessary? When workers report a hazard, does it trigger punishment or problem-solving?

Safety Differently doesn't mean abandoning standards or accepting whatever workers decide to do. It means recognizing that your crew isn't executing a script — they're improvising within constraints, and their improvisations contain expertise your formal system doesn't capture.

Mascaro's partnership with regulators suggests this shift is possible even within traditional compliance frameworks. The Alabama and Revoli incidents show what happens when it doesn't occur. Repeat violations don't reveal defiant workers. They reveal organizations that keep writing rules instead of learning how work actually happens.

What if your next safety meeting started with "show me how you solved that problem yesterday" instead of "here's the new rule after last week's incident"? What would your crew tell you about how they keep work safe that isn't in any procedure manual?
