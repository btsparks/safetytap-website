## The Question Nobody Wants to Ask

A worker falls through an unprotected floor opening on the third deck. Ambulance leaves. OSHA gets called. Your safety manager pulls out the incident investigation form and starts checking boxes.

The investigation finds the worker wasn't clipped in. He'd been told about the opening in the morning meeting. The form recommends retraining on fall protection and reminds everyone to stay alert. Case closed. Except nothing actually changed, and six months later someone nearly goes through a different opening on a different project.

The investigation answered "what happened" but never touched "why it made sense at the time." And that's where the real learning lives.

## The Alabama Trench That Killed Someone

The Department of Labor recently investigated an Alabama construction company after a worker died in a trench collapse. The investigation found workers regularly entering unprotected excavations over five feet deep. No shoring. No protective systems. Multiple workers exposed, multiple days.

The standard investigation response would focus on the worker who entered the trench. He knew better. He'd been trained. Why didn't he refuse?

But organizational learning researcher John Carroll studied incidents in high-hazard industries and found that effective learning requires examining the logic that made unsafe actions seem reasonable at the time. The worker entered that trench because the system around him made it the path of least resistance. Someone approved the work plan. Someone allocated insufficient time or equipment. Someone created schedule pressure that made shortcuts feel necessary. The trench didn't suddenly become unsafe the day someone died. The conditions developed over time while the organization looked away.

## When Violations Become Willful

A Florida roofing company was cited for willful violations after one worker died from a fall and another was injured. Willful means OSHA found evidence the company knew about the hazards and ignored them. Not ignorance — active disregard.

But here's what most incident investigations miss: willful violations don't appear overnight. They develop through dozens of small organizational decisions that normalize risk. Sidney Dekker, who studies how organizations understand error, calls this the local rationality principle. At each moment, the actions made sense to the people involved given what they knew, what they were trying to accomplish, and what pressures they faced.

The roofing company didn't wake up one day and decide to kill someone. They made incremental decisions — skip the anchor point here, work unprotected there, prioritize schedule over setup — and each decision felt justified in context. The investigation that matters asks: what organizational conditions made those decisions feel reasonable? What production targets existed? How were supervisors evaluated? What happened the last time someone stopped work for safety?

## The Multi-Hazard Problem

Recent analysis of roofing work environments found workers managing simultaneous hazards — heat, heights, heavy equipment, constantly changing work surfaces. That's not a training problem. That's cognitive overload baked into the work structure.

When your incident investigation concludes with "worker should have been more aware," you've missed the point. Peter Senge's work on learning organizations emphasizes systems thinking — understanding how structure drives behavior. If workers are managing six hazards at once while meeting aggressive production schedules with inadequate crew sizes, the structure guarantees someone will eventually miss something. The investigation should examine who designed that structure and why it persists.

## What an Actual Investigation Looks Like

Go back to that floor opening. The effective investigation starts with the same facts but asks different questions.

Why was the opening unprotected? The covers were stored two floors down because the material hoist was broken and nobody wanted to carry them up three times a day. Who decided not to fix the hoist? Procurement said the part would take two weeks. Who decided that was acceptable? The superintendent was already behind schedule from weather delays. Who created the schedule with no weather buffer?

Now you're learning. Now you're finding the organizational decisions that made the incident possible. And now everyone gets uncomfortable, which is the point. As Carroll's research showed, organizations that learn from incidents develop the capability to make themselves uncomfortable by examining their own logic.

The investigation should document competing priorities. It should identify which production targets conflicted with safety measures. It should name the organizational trade-offs that were made and who made them. It should feel less like a worker's failing and more like a systems autopsy.

## The Evidence You Need

The problem with most investigations: they rely on memory, conducted under pressure, filtered through fear of blame. Workers reconstruct what they think they remember. Supervisors describe what they believe happened. Everyone's story aligns with what they wish had been true.

You need objective evidence of what conditions actually existed before the incident. What did the site look like two days before? What hazards were developing? What informal workarounds had become normal? Photos from the days leading up create an evidence trail that supports honest investigation. They show the system conditions that made the incident possible rather than just the final moment when something broke.

An investigation anchored in visual evidence can ask: what does this photo from Tuesday tell us about how work was actually organized? Why did this condition exist for three days without intervention? What does that reveal about our inspection systems, our reporting culture, our response capability?

## What Changes After

The goal isn't assigning blame. The goal is surfacing the organizational logic that needs to change. If your investigation ends with retraining the worker, you learned nothing. If it ends with uncomfortable questions about scheduling practices, staffing decisions, procurement delays, and competing priorities, you might prevent the next one.

Does your incident review process make the organization uncomfortable, or just the worker who got hurt?
