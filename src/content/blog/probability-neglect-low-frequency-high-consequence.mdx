The trench is six feet deep. The walls look solid — same soil you've been working in all week. Yesterday went fine. The day before that, too. You need to lay another twenty feet of pipe before lunch, and the shoring equipment is back at the yard. Someone says, "We'll be in and out in fifteen minutes."

What are the chances something goes wrong in the next fifteen minutes?

In March 2024, OSHA investigated a fatal trench collapse in Alabama. The crew was working in sandy soil without protective systems. In another incident that same spring, investigators found Revoli Construction with seven willful violations and thirty-three repeat violations after a worker died in an unsupported trench. These weren't freak accidents. The conditions were visible. The regulations were clear. The equipment existed.

So why did experienced professionals make the decision to enter?

## The Probability Trap

Cass Sunstein, a legal scholar who studied how people assess catastrophic risks, identified something he called probability neglect. When people think about low-probability, high-consequence events, they don't process the numbers rationally. They either fixate on the terrible outcome — treating it as certain and panicking — or they dismiss the probability entirely, rounding it down to zero in their minds.

In construction, the second response dominates. A trench collapse on any single day has a genuinely low probability. Your crew has dug hundreds of trenches. Most days, nothing happens. The soil looks stable. The math appears to support the crew's intuition: the probability today feels like zero.

Daniel Kahneman explored this in his research on how humans misjudge probability. We're terrible at distinguishing between "very unlikely" and "impossible." Our brains prefer certainty. We treat small probabilities as if they don't exist, especially when we've gotten away with something multiple times before. Each successful day without an incident reinforces the belief that the risk isn't real.

But here's the thing about low-probability events: they're only low-probability per exposure. Multiply that tiny daily risk by hundreds of trench entries across a project, across a season, across a company's annual work, and the math changes completely. What feels impossible on Tuesday becomes statistically inevitable somewhere, sometime.

## Why Experience Makes It Worse

You'd think experienced crews would be better at assessing these risks. They're not. They're often worse.

The affect heuristic, described by Paul Slovic and his colleagues, explains why. People judge risk based on how they feel about something, not on statistical reasoning. If your experience with trenches is mostly positive — you've done this a hundred times without incident — then trenches feel safe. Your emotional response overrides the data.

The Alabama case isn't an outlier. Neither is Revoli Construction, despite the thirty-three repeat violations. These aren't isolated failures by reckless crews. They're predictable outcomes of how human brains process rare events under time pressure with competing priorities.

Work zones on highways show the same pattern. During National Work Zone Awareness Week earlier this year, industry leaders pushed for renewed focus on work zone safety as the surface transportation bill comes up for renewal. Awareness campaigns have run for years. Everyone knows work zones are dangerous. Drivers see the signs. Workers wear high-visibility gear. And yet crashes keep happening because both drivers and workers underestimate the probability of the specific moment when a vehicle loses control or drifts into the zone.

Knowing something is dangerous in general is not the same as believing it's dangerous right now. Probability neglect doesn't respond to statistics or reminders. It responds to immediate, concrete conditions.

## The Duration Problem

Construction projects stretch across weeks, months, sometimes years. A low daily probability becomes a near certainty over that duration. But crews don't think in project timelines. They think in shift timelines, task timelines, "we just need to finish this one section" timelines.

A trench that has a one-in-a-thousand chance of collapsing during any given entry sounds safe for today's work. But if your crew makes fifty trench entries over a project, and your company runs ten similar projects a year, you're no longer talking about one-in-a-thousand odds. You're talking about an event that is likely to happen, just not predictably when.

This is why repeat violations persist. Revoli Construction's thirty-three repeat violations weren't evidence of ignorance. They were evidence that the company kept getting away with it — until they didn't. Each successful day reinforced the belief that the regulations were overly cautious, that the real probability was even lower than the already-low number, that "it won't happen to us."

Organizational learning fails because the system rewards the gamble most of the time. The crew that skips shoring saves two hours. They finish faster. They move to the next job. The reinforcement is immediate and positive. The consequence, if it comes, is distant and unpredictable.

## From Probability to Conditions

The way out of probability neglect isn't better probability education. You can't teach someone to feel differently about one-in-a-thousand odds when their gut says zero and their schedule says hurry.

The way out is to stop thinking in probabilities at all.

Tension cracks in a trench wall aren't a probability. They're a present condition. Sandy soil isn't a statistical risk. It's a material fact. A trench deeper than five feet without shoring isn't a chance of collapse. It's a violation of physics waiting to assert itself.

When you shift from "what are the chances" to "what do I see right now," probability neglect loses its grip. You're no longer asking your brain to assess invisible statistics. You're asking it to observe visible conditions. This is something human brains are actually good at — when they're prompted to look.

The Alabama crew that died in the trench wasn't killed by probability. They were killed by present conditions: sandy soil, no protective system, a depth that exceeded safe limits. Those conditions existed before anyone entered. They were observable. They just weren't observed with the right frame.

## What Gets Noticed

Every crew has someone who's good at seeing problems before they become incidents. That person isn't doing complex probability math. They're noticing details. The way the soil looks different today than yesterday. The crack that wasn't there this morning. The equipment that's positioned wrong.

SafetyTAP works by making that observation process external and structured. A crew texts a photo of their trench. They get back a specific observation about present conditions — the kind an experienced pro would notice on site. Not "there's a 3.7% chance of collapse," but "the trench wall shows tension cracks at the south end."

That's not a probability statement. It's a fact captured in a photo. It sidesteps the entire mental trap of probability neglect because it's not asking anyone to assess likelihood. It's just pointing at what's there.

The question isn't "will this trench collapse today?" The question is "what does this trench look like right now?" One question spirals into probability neglect and wishful thinking. The other question demands observation.

It won't happen to us — until the present conditions make it certain.
