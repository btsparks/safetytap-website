The super calls you over. "We need to talk about your crew. I'm seeing too many unsafe behaviors in the weekly observation reports. Get them retrained on fall protection." You walk back to the site knowing your crew clips in every time. The real problem? The anchor points are in the wrong spots, so workers have to unclip and move to reach half the work area. But the observation form doesn't have a box for that.

This is Heinrich's domino theory at work, nearly a century after its creation.

## The Model That Won't Die

In 1931, Herbert William Heinrich published Industrial Accident Prevention and introduced what became the most influential safety model in history: the domino theory. Heinrich proposed that incidents follow a linear sequence — ancestry and social environment lead to personal faults, which lead to unsafe acts or conditions, which cause accidents, which result in injuries. Remove one domino, prevent the chain reaction.

Most importantly, Heinrich claimed that 88 percent of incidents were caused by unsafe acts of workers themselves. The implications were clear: fix the workers, prevent the incidents.

Construction ate this up. It fit perfectly with how we already thought about safety. Someone gets hurt? Find out what they did wrong. Write it up. Retrain them. Count behaviors. The system stayed the same, but we had numbers to report.

The problem is that Heinrich was wrong about almost everything that mattered.

## What the Data Actually Said

Fred Manuele went back and examined Heinrich's original research in detail for a 2011 paper reviewing the foundations of modern safety practice. The famous 88 percent figure? It came from a dataset that Heinrich himself acknowledged was based on "the general opinions of supervisors." Not incident investigations. Not root cause analysis. Supervisor opinions about why things went wrong.

Heinrich also excluded entire categories of incidents from his analysis, particularly those involving equipment and process failures. When you remove system failures from your dataset before you start counting, you'll naturally conclude that people cause most problems.

Sidney Dekker put it more bluntly in The Field Guide to Understanding Human Error: the old view of human error is "empirically bankrupt." Incidents don't follow neat linear chains. They emerge from the interaction of multiple system factors, most of which are invisible until something goes wrong.

Look at the Alabama trenching fatality OSHA investigated recently. The domino model asks: what unsafe act did the worker commit? But the investigative findings point to a company-level decision not to implement basic trenching protections. No cave-in protection system. No competent person conducting daily inspections. The worker who died wasn't the problem — the entire organizational approach to that hazard was the problem.

Or consider the Florida roofing company cited for willful violations after one worker died from a fall and another was injured. OSHA used the word "willful" — meaning the company knew about the hazard and consciously chose to expose workers anyway. This wasn't a behavior problem. It was a management decision that production mattered more than protection. The domino model has no place for that kind of causation because it keeps the focus down at the worker level.

## Why We Keep Using a Broken Model

The domino theory survives because it's simple and it protects the system. If 88 percent of incidents come from worker behavior, then 88 percent of your safety effort should focus on correcting workers. You can fill out observation checklists. You can discipline people. You can report impressive numbers to ownership.

What you can't do is question why the schedule doesn't allow time to set up proper access. You can't ask why the safety equipment specified in the plan isn't the safety equipment that actually works for this task. You can't examine how production pressure creates the conditions where cutting corners becomes the norm.

The Human and Organizational Performance movement, now gaining traction in construction, flips this completely. HOP starts from the position that human error is inevitable and that systems should be designed to tolerate it. When something goes wrong, the question isn't "who screwed up" but "what about our system made this outcome possible?"

That's a harder conversation. It requires looking at management decisions, resource allocation, schedule pressure, and equipment design. It means admitting that the company's choices create the conditions where workers face impossible trade-offs.

## What Changes When You Drop the Dominoes

A superintendent using the Heinrich model sees a worker on a ladder without three points of contact and writes up an unsafe behavior. A superintendent using modern safety science sees the same situation and asks why that particular task requires a ladder in the first place, whether the work could be done from a proper platform, and what schedule pressure might be driving the shortcut.

The first approach feels productive. You identified a problem, documented it, and took corrective action. The second approach feels messy because it leads to questions about the project setup itself.

But only the second approach actually reduces serious incident risk. The research is overwhelmingly clear on this: complex work systems fail due to the interaction of organizational, technical, and human factors. Individual behavior is almost never the root cause. It's the visible symptom of system design.

When your safety program is built on counting behaviors and correcting individuals, you're running a 1931 operating system on 2025 hazards. The serious incidents keep happening not because you're missing unsafe acts, but because you're looking at the wrong level entirely.

The question isn't whether your workers are following the rules. The question is whether your system makes following the rules actually possible when the schedule is tight, the equipment is wrong, and the work area doesn't match the plan. That's where incidents come from, and that's where they have to be prevented.
