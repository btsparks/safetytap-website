The crew had been at it since 6 a.m. — two hours of hauling sections, locking pins, leveling base plates on uneven ground. By the time the foreman walked over with the scope sheet, the scaffold was up, tagged, and tied off. Then he looked at the actual work: overhead penetrations, four feet deeper than anyone thought, requiring repositioning and a cantilever setup. The scaffold they built wouldn't work. Everyone knew it. But the pushback was immediate: "We've already got it set up. We can make it work."

Two hours were already gone. The scaffold couldn't be moved without tearing half of it down. And the crew had other work stacking up behind this. The rational decision was obvious — reconfigure now, do it right. But that's not how the decision felt. It felt like throwing away two hours of work. So they tried to make it work.

## The Cost That Shouldn't Matter

What happened on that site is one of the most studied phenomena in decision science, and one of the least recognized on jobsites. Psychologist Hal Arkes and Catherine Blumer ran experiments in the 1980s showing that people consistently factor in costs that are already gone when making decisions about what to do next. They called it the sunk cost effect. A person who has already spent money, time, or effort on something will continue with it — even when stopping would be smarter — because they can't let that investment "go to waste."

Rationally, sunk costs should be irrelevant. The two hours spent building the scaffold are gone whether the crew uses it or tears it down. The only question that matters is: what setup gives us the best outcome from this point forward? But humans don't think that way under pressure. We treat past investment as a reason to continue. The more we've already put in, the harder it is to walk away.

Economist Richard Thaler described this as the Concorde effect, after the supersonic jet program that kept getting funded long after it was clear it would never be profitable. Governments had already spent so much that stopping felt like admitting failure. Construction sites run on the same psychology. The longer a setup takes, the harder it becomes to call it inadequate.

## When 'Relatively Safe' Becomes the Trap

The sunk cost fallacy doesn't just apply to scaffolds. It shows up anywhere a crew has invested effort into a process or setup that turns out to be riskier than expected. The Chemical Safety Board's recent investigation into a fatal explosion at a Tennessee facility highlighted a version of this. The facility handled explosives described as "relatively insensitive" — a term that sounds reassuring. Over time, that language created comfort. Workers developed processes around the assumption that these materials were stable under normal handling. But "relatively insensitive" is not the same as safe. The materials could still ignite under friction, heat, or impact.

Once procedures were established and crews were trained on them, changing course required admitting that the original assumptions were wrong. The company had already invested in those procedures. Workers had learned them. Challenging the setup felt like second-guessing everything that had come before. The sunk cost wasn't just time — it was institutional commitment. So the procedures stayed, and the reassuring language stayed, until conditions proved they shouldn't have.

This is how sunk costs operate in high-risk environments. The investment isn't always financial. Sometimes it's reputational. Sometimes it's the cognitive effort of learning a process. Sometimes it's just the hours already burned. Whatever form it takes, it creates resistance to changing course, even when the current course is inadequate.

## Escalation Under Production Pressure

Barry Staw studied this pattern in organizational settings and found that commitment escalates when people feel personally responsible for the initial decision. He described it as getting "knee-deep in the big muddy" — the more you've waded in, the harder it is to turn back, even as conditions worsen. On a construction site, this shows up as crews doubling down on inadequate setups rather than stopping to rebuild.

The foreman who approved the original scaffold configuration feels the pressure. Admitting it won't work means admitting he didn't catch it earlier. The crew feels it too. They spent two hours. Tearing it down feels like their time didn't matter. So everyone looks for a way to make it work. They add a plank. They reposition the load. They tell themselves it's close enough. The original investment — time, effort, face — becomes the lens through which every subsequent decision gets made.

Production pressure amplifies this. When there's a schedule to hit and other trades waiting, stopping feels expensive. But stopping isn't the expensive part. The expensive part already happened when the scaffold went up wrong. Continuing from that point just adds risk on top of wasted time. But it doesn't feel that way in the moment. It feels like salvaging the investment.

## The Voice That Doesn't Care What You Already Spent

Breaking the sunk cost pattern requires an external reference point — something that evaluates the current setup without regard for how much effort went into it. That's difficult for people embedded in the situation. The foreman knows how long it took. The crew knows how hard they worked. Everyone has context, and context makes it harder to see clearly.

An outside perspective doesn't carry that baggage. It looks at what's there now and assesses it against what the task actually requires. That's the role SafetyTAP plays when a crew texts in a photo. The system evaluates the setup in the image without knowing or caring how long it took to build. If the scaffold configuration isn't adequate for the task, the feedback reflects that. The two hours spent setting it up aren't part of the calculation.

That kind of input cuts through the emotional weight of sunk costs. It's not about blame. It's not about wasted effort. It's just an objective read of current conditions. And that makes it easier for the crew to do what they already knew was right — reconfigure and move forward.

## What Matters Is What Happens Next

Sunk costs are everywhere on jobsites. The trench box that took an hour to position. The access route that's mostly clear. The rigging plan that's almost right. Crews are constantly making decisions about whether to continue with an imperfect setup or stop and rebuild. The investment already made shouldn't factor into that decision, but it does. Every time.

The question isn't whether your crew feels the pull of sunk costs. They do. The question is whether they have a way to cut through it when it matters.
